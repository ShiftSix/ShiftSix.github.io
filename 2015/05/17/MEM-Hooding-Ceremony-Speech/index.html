MEM Hooding Ceremony Speech

By Lian.Liu

May 17 2015 Updated:Sep 25 2015

Contents

Was designated to be the MEM Hooding Ceremony speaker of my classmates at Duke University. Special Thanks to Staci Thornton and Alec Weisman for the suggestions and proofreading.
Speech Video

Good afternoon. Distinguished guests, faculty and staff, family and friends, thank you for coming today, to show your support for us and our achievements! Congratulations, my dear classmates! It’s an honor to be standing up here to review the past and look forward. Thank you for giving me this opportunity…..I am sure you won’t regret it.
What makes MEM special, the Master of Engineering Management program? I invite you to look back at the year together with me. Yes, please join me! (the following section involves some interactions with the audience) Who said “if I don’t see you at seminars, you are gonna see me.”? (Dr. Murray). Yes, we have the most conscientious and generous staff and faculty. Thank you, Dr. Glass, Dr. Fox, Dr. Murray, for bringing us an insightful, challenging and enjoyable program. Who has been married for 13,314 days till today? (CJ Skender) Yes, we have the most experienced and charming professors. Thank you, to all of the professors…for providing us with a vivid perspective of the industry. Your dedication to teaching doesn’t stop in the classroom, but continues through lunches and coffee hours. Do you still remember our very first Guide to Durham, with all the interesting places to eat, to do and to buy? (Bridget) And who always brought dogs to MEM suite during the intense final weeks? (Lorelle) yes, we have an amazing and trustworthy staff. Thank you, Staci, Susan, Erin, Jamila, Bridget, and Lorelle, for making us feel like a part of this big family, and a part of a winning team. Who has read every version of our resumes and cover letters? (Jenny, etc.) Yes, we have the most reliable career service team. Thank you, Jenny, Leah, Christina, for being available, helpful and encouraging all the time on our career path.
Friday afternoon is our favorite time at MEM. Not only because of the food from all around the world, which is important, but also because of the insightful seminars and splendid cultural presentations. Not only do we have the opportunity to gain close connections with industry experts, but also to learn cultural diversity from each other. The rest of the week is delightful as well. We spent three months running a medicine company; went ecstatic or crazy with the up and down of the stock price. We successfully led a chaotic team to complete the project in time even though our poor boss Ana left the company with 200 failed projects. We helped Ella and Tomas deal with all the legal issues when they launched their love bubble company. The courses were sometimes frustrating; the deadlines were always one after another, but in the end they made us feel accomplished.
Looking forward, I’d like to share with you three things about choice, which I learned from MEM, and will be invaluable for the future.
Staci always says, I can do nothing if you find me after the course add and drop ends. The choice of courses determines our sleep quality, happiness index, and—to some extent—our final grades. It is important but we only have two weeks to make the choice. If you miss the deadline, not even Staci can help you. So seize the day and make your choice! Opportunities are passing by never to return. If you meet someone friendly and charming, don’t hesitate; go and talk to him, or her. Because they will leave and you may not see them again. that is an advice for attending career fairs. We are afforded many opportunities in life… take advantage and make the choices in time.
The second thing I want to leave us today is that there is no need to regret the choices you have made. In our first seminar, Jason McMain said “to plan is worthwhile but the plan itself is worthless”. This is the fascination of our time. Things change so quickly that we can no longer predict what will happen next. A bad choice now may turn into an opportunity later. Glancing down and regretting on the past will only blind you from what lies ahead. Just like sitting in class, drifting away for only two minutes, you will feel completely lost about the concept. But if you then try to go back and start again, you will miss the whole class and will need more than three hours to make up what you missed. What if we really make some bad choices? In the PharmaSim Simulation, we probably all make some…questionable decisions, reducing the price, killing a product, spending money on meaningless tests. Instead of blaming each other or regretting it, we always analyzed and reported the data in order to find better solutions. We need to convert bad choices into opportunities.
Last but not the least, your choices deserve respect; so do others. One of the most distinguishing aspects of the MEM program is that we learn from engineers with background in all kinds of industries and cultures. We argued fiercely in class discussion. We had tears in team projects. But agreeing to disagree is the catalyst of progress. Outside the classroom, we have been able to collect on the dividends of diversity. Our classmates invited us to parties for Diwali; we had mooncakes for the Mid-autumn Festival. We celebrated New Year’s Day together; learned French, Russian and Spanish. We explain to each other the meanings of our names. This experience with you has taught me to embrace our differences. So, even when I cannot understand the choices made by others, I learn to respect it.
Life is a one-way journey. Our choices enlighten the way. Some choices lead us to spectacular scenery; some bring us barriers and ambiguities. But we never know what will happen next. What we can do is to learn from experience and make better choices. MEM has equipped us with the appreciation of sceneries, the knowledge to turn barriers into opportunities and the courage to face ambiguities. While our beautiful, wonderful, magical time here at Duke is coming to an end, a new journey is waiting for us just outside that door. As engineers and future leaders, we are prepared for the challenging choices that lie ahead of us.
Now for our memories slideshow at MEM put together by Kruti Jain.
